Design and analysis of a low-cost gripper with compliant, tendon-driven continuum fingers

Avni Mahajan
Delhi, India
Paper CAD

Presentation Video

We introduce a low cost (under $20) tendon-driven gripper built using underactuated continuum fingers with spring backbones. This gripper demonstrates mechanical intelligence and compliance, allowing it to handle a wide variety of objects of different sizes, shapes, textures, compressibility, and weights.

The continuum backbone of the finger allows the finger to passively conform to the shape of the object being grasped. When the servo motor pulls the tendon, it exerts a force on the uppermost disk, causing it to tilt in the direction of the force applied. Consequently, the spring below is stretched and exerts a restoring force on the disk below, causing it to tilt as well. This sequential bending continues down the structure until the last disk, which remains fixed to the base. Any external force applied to the disks by an object constrains their motion, altering the spring bending pattern and modifying the trajectory of the finger as seen in the video

Size Experiment

Intuitively it was expected that the gripper would be able to grip the smallest object with ease. Unexpectedly, it was observed that the smallest( 4 cm) prism had the lowest success rate. This drop in performance can be attributed to the adduction exhibited by the fingers, but unexpected results, as shown in Fig.6, were observed where the smallest( 4 cm) prism had the lowest success rate. This drop in performance can be attributed to the adduction exhibited by the fingers in this case. It was observed that the adduction was significantly less for the medium (8 cm) and large (12 cm) prisms. This makes the design particularly effective when gripping objects larger than the initial gap between the fingers. As the object pushes the fingers outward, a restoring force is generated, which enhances grip strength and improves the gripper’s stability when handling larger items.

Morphology Experiment

The gripper's performance was evaluated against real world objects of different shapes, sizes, textures and compressability. The grippper exhibited strong performance with majority of the objects but had trouble manipulating thin cylinderical objects like a pen. This is atttributed to the absence of fingertrips which would help with manipulating thin objects
